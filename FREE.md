# Free Used to Mean Free

*A practitioner's note on what search algorithms did to a word — and a commons.*

---

"Free" used to mean no cost, no strings. It was a founding principle of the early web — baked into the GNU project's four freedoms, into Creative Commons, into the culture that built Project Gutenberg, Newgrounds, Jamendo, and the original open-source ecosystem.

That word has been systematically hollowed out.

## What happened

Google's economic model requires that every query be a monetization opportunity. When a user searches for "free tarot reading," the algorithm does not try to find genuinely free tarot readings. It identifies a user with low budget and high intent, and surfaces monetization funnels — per-minute psychic services, freemium trials, AI upsell apps — optimized by companies with large SEO budgets that a volunteer-run site cannot match.

This is not a bug. It is the architecture.

The same pattern runs across every domain:

- **Divination**: Platforms like Kasamba offer "3 free minutes" to hook users into $10–$20/minute psychic services. Sites like Tarotsmith — genuinely free since 2009, no upsell, no bait — are algorithmically invisible by comparison.
- **Software**: Searches for "free software" surface Softonic and Download.com — platforms with documented histories of adware bundling and deceptive installers — above GNU.org and legitimate open-source repositories.
- **Music**: "Free MP3 download" now leads primarily to malware traps and paywalled streaming services. Jamendo, Internet Archive, and Creative Commons netlabels are buried.
- **Education**: Coursera and Udemy dominate "free online course" results. Their free content is engineered lead generation for expensive certifications. Khan Academy and MIT OpenCourseWare — genuinely free, no funnel — rank lower.
- **Ebooks**: "Free ebook" surfaces Kindle spam, subscription traps, and AI-stuffed fake download sites. Project Gutenberg's 75,000 public domain works are not the top result.
- **Games**: "Free game" is now a synonym for predatory freemium mechanics — loot boxes, energy meters, pay-to-win economies. The indie game commons that once existed on itch.io and Newgrounds is effectively invisible to new users via search.

## The recursive problem

LLMs are trained on web content and SERP data. If Google's results for "free X" are predominantly freemium and bait-and-switch, then AI models learn that this is what "free X" means. They reproduce the distortion in their recommendations. The corruption is self-reinforcing.

## What this costs

The obvious cost is financial: users are repeatedly deceived, trust erodes, and the practitioners who built genuinely free resources cannot survive the traffic starvation.

The less obvious cost is epistemic. When the infrastructure of information access systematically misrepresents a foundational concept, it shapes what users believe is possible. It teaches a generation that "free" always conceals a trap — and in doing so, it makes genuine generosity illegible.

The digital commons is not a sentimental idea. It was a real, functioning ecosystem. Search algorithms did not destroy it accidentally.
